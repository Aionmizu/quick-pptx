SLIDE-DESIGN GUIDELINES — Naegle KM, "Ten simple rules for effective
presentation slides," PLOS Comp Bio (2021). doi:10.1371/journal.pcbi.1009554

Apply these on top of the project's design philosophy. They constrain
content density and cognitive load — they DO NOT prevent rich composition.

Rule 1 — One idea per slide.
  Each slide has exactly one central message. If a slide tries to deliver
  two ideas, split it.

Rule 2 — One minute per slide (≈30s for very minimal density).
  If the content can't be presented spoken in ~1 min, it's too dense.

Rule 3 — The heading is the message.
  The slide title states the slide's CONCLUSION, not its TOPIC.
  WRONG: "Results"            RIGHT: "False-positive rates depend on sample size"
  WRONG: "Background"          RIGHT: "SH2 domains bind phosphorylated tyrosines"

Rule 4 — Essential points only.
  If a detail isn't going to be discussed, remove it. Eyes wander to anything
  visible — every element earns its place.

Rule 5 — Credit, where due.
  When sources matter, place them consistently (typically a small line at
  the foot). Cite when the user provided sources; don't fabricate citations.

Rule 6 — Use graphics effectively.
  Almost never have text-only slides. A picture is worth a thousand words.
  But: don't muddy the point with too many complex graphics on a single
  slide. One visual concept per slide.

Rule 7 — Avoid cognitive overload — ≤6 informational elements per slide.
  - No full sentences in the body. Use short fragments / guideposts.
  - Sans-serif body (already enforced by tokens). Avoid italics and
    underline as primary cues; prefer bold for emphasis.
  - High contrast text/background.
  - Color is never the sole signal.
  Decorative shapes (accent bars, separators, ornaments) DO NOT count
  toward the 6-element limit — only informational elements do.

Rule 8 — The distracted-person test.
  If someone glances at the slide for 2 seconds without hearing what was
  said, would they grasp the main point? If not, the slide fails.
  - Title states the conclusion (Rule 3).
  - Body supports it.
  - No shiny baubles distracting from the message.

Rule 9 — Iterate through practice. (User-facing rule.)
  The generated deck is a starting point. The user should read through,
  edit, and rehearse. Don't enforce this in the slides themselves.

Rule 10 — Mitigate technical disasters.
  Avoid animations, fly-ins, and movement effects. They are universally
  disliked, create accessibility issues, and break across tools.
  Stick to static slides with strong typography and layout.

OPERATIONAL TRANSLATION
- Density: minimal → 1–3 informational elements per slide;
           medium → 3–5; dense → ≤6 (never exceed).
- Titles: write the conclusion sentence.
- Body: short fragments, not sentences (Rule 7).
- Composition: vary layout for visual rhythm (Rule 6) — Rule 7 limits
  TEXT, not visual variety.
